Unit anatomy — tap hotspots to identify each section
① Filter section
16× 4″ MERV 8 filters in a 4-wide × 4-tall grid. This is the face of the unit that you see in the mechanical gallery — filters are front-loaded and can be pulled and replaced while the unit is running, without any isolation required. Warm air drawn from the gallery contacts the filters first, before reaching the coil or fans. A differential pressure transducer monitors filter loading and alarms at 0.81 inH₂O.
② Fan array section
9× EBM-Pabst M3G150 ECM fans in a 3×3 grid, behind the filter bank. 415V, 3-phase, 4,400 W each at max load, 1,700 RPM. All nine run at the same speed, modulated 30–100% by the BACnet controller. A single fan failure does not shut the unit. Each fan reports its own run status — individual fan faults appear on the HMI Fans screen.
③ Cooling coil section
Chilled water (27% glycol / 73% water) enters and exits via top manifold connections. A Belimo EPIV valve modulates flow to maintain the discharge air temperature setpoint (default 55°F). The stainless drain pan runs the full width at the base. The rope leak detector sits in the drain pan on the mechanical gallery floor. The EPIV is accessible from the mechanical gallery side.
④ Electrical & controls panel
On a right-hand unit, the electrical panel is on the right side of the unit when facing it from the mechanical gallery. The 7″ Loytec HMI touchscreen is mounted on the panel door, visible and operable from the gallery. The BACnet controller, UPS (controller only — not fans), and single-point power connection are all housed here. Field software changes void the factory warranty.

Key components — tap any card for detail
▦
MERV 8 filters
4″ pleated · 16 per unit · front-load
Arranged in a 4-wide × 4-tall grid on the mechanical gallery face of the unit. Because they are front-loaded and face into the gallery, filters can be pulled and replaced while the unit is fully operational — no isolation required. This is a deliberate design feature: the filter bank is the first thing the return air contacts, keeping the coil fins clean. A Setra differential pressure transducer monitors resistance across the filter bank. The alarm threshold is 0.81 inH₂O sustained for 5 minutes. The alarm self-resets when DP drops below setpoint. On rounds, inspect filters visually for physical damage, heavy loading, or collapse.
◎
ECM fan motors
EBM-Pabst M3G150 · 9 per unit
Nine fans in a 3×3 array behind the filter bank. Each is an Electronically-Commutated Motor (ECM) — a brushless variable-speed motor that maintains good efficiency across its speed range. 415V, 3-phase, 560 mm diameter, 5-blade aluminum impeller, 4,400 W and 1,700 RPM at max load, IP55 rated. All nine are commanded to the same speed by the BACnet controller. Each fan independently reports a dry-contact run status signal back to the controller — individual fan faults appear as SF1–SF9 mismatch alarms on the HMI. A single fan failure does not shut the unit down. On rounds, listen for abnormal noise or vibration from the fan section, which can indicate a bearing issue or mechanical fault.
≋
Chilled water coil
Top-connect · 27/73 glycol mix
The coil sits behind the fan array and spans the full cross-section of the unit. Chilled water supply and return connections are both at the top of the unit. The fluid is a 27% glycol / 73% water mixture circulated by the building's Chilled Water Pumps (CHWPs) from the 24 rooftop air-cooled chillers. Glycol is present to protect the system against freezing in cold conditions. Maximum system pressure is 130 psi; maximum fluid temperature 95°F. Do not use chemical cleaners on the coil — clean fresh water only. After any flushing, vent trapped air and check all strainers.
⊛
EPIV control valve
Belimo · 2-way · pressure-independent
The Electronically-Pressure-Independent Valve (EPIV) controls how much chilled water flows through the coil. Unlike a standard valve, the EPIV contains an ultrasonic flow meter and delivers the precise commanded flow rate regardless of pressure fluctuations elsewhere in the building's chilled water system. This matters because all 21 fan walls and other building loads share the same distribution loop — pressure can vary significantly. The valve is located inside the unit on the coil section and is accessible from the mechanical gallery. It is modulated by the BACnet controller based on discharge air temperature. The BAS must never command this valve directly — it is always under local controller PID control.
⊞
BACnet controller & HMI
Loytec LVIS-3ME7 · Version 4
The Building Management System (BMS) — the facility-wide control platform — communicates with each fan wall unit via BACnet/IP, an open building controls protocol. The unit controller (Loytec LVIS-3ME7, running Metasys Version 4 software) manages fan speed, valve modulation, and all alarms. The 7″ HMI touchscreen is mounted on the electrical panel door, facing the mechanical gallery, and is the local interface for operators and technicians. All users can view any HMI screen without logging in. Responding to alarms and monitoring unit status does not require a login. Adjusting setpoints requires the service PIN (9675) and is a task for authorized personnel. Field software changes void the factory warranty.
⬕
Drain pan & leak detector
Stainless · full coil width · rope sensor
A stainless steel drain pan runs the full width under the cooling coil, capturing any condensate produced when the cold coil meets warm humid return air. Below the unit on the mechanical gallery floor sits a bathtub-style secondary drain pan — this is also where the rope-style leak detector cable is located. During rounds, check the drain pan for standing water. A small amount of condensate draining normally is expected; pooled standing water is not and should be investigated and escalated. The rope leak detector connected to the controller will trigger a floor leak alarm if moisture is detected — respond to this immediately.
Engineering & controls — how the system works

The building cooling chain

The fan wall units are the final link in the building's chilled water cooling system. Understanding the chain from roof to rack puts their operation in context.

Rooftop air-cooled chillers (ACCs)

The 24 rooftop units produce the chilled water that ultimately cools the data hall. They can operate in three modes depending on outside air temperature: free cooling (integral fans reject building heat directly to cold outdoor air, no refrigerant required), mechanical cooling (R-134a refrigerant compressors handle the load on warm days), or a combined mode using both. The ACCs serve the entire building — the fan walls are one of several loads on the chilled water loop. The full ACC operation is covered in the separate chiller guide.

Chilled Water Pumps (CHWPs) & the glycol mix

The CHWPs circulate a 27% glycol / 73% water mixture from the ACCs to all building loads, including the 21 fan walls. Glycol is added to protect the system against freezing. The loop is closed and pressurised — warm fluid returns from the building loads back to the ACCs to be re-chilled continuously.

Why the air loop is closed — humidity control

Hot exhaust air from the server racks is contained in the hot aisles (enclosed with sliding glass doors) and directed via a return plenum back to the mechanical gallery, where it is drawn back into the fan wall units. This closed air loop means the facility is not continuously ingesting outside air at unpredictable humidity levels. Recirculating the same air mass makes it far easier to maintain stable humidity in the data hall without constant humidification or dehumidification correction.

How the unit controls temperature

Each fan wall unit independently controls its discharge air temperature. The BACnet controller reads the discharge air temperature sensors and modulates the EPIV valve to drive the air temperature toward the setpoint (default 55°F). When the discharge air is warmer than setpoint, the valve opens further to increase chilled water flow. When it is at or below setpoint, the valve modulates closed.

Important for operators: The cooling valve is always under the unit's own control — in all operating modes. If you see a discharge temperature alarm, the correct response is to check the HMI for valve output percentage and chilled water flow, and escalate if the unit is not responding. Do not attempt to command the valve from the BAS or HMI directly.

If the discharge air temperature sensors fail, the controller enters a safe state and drives the valve to a configured failure position (default: closed). This will appear on the HMI as a sensor failure alarm alongside a discharge temperature alarm — escalate immediately.

Fan speed control — Local vs Remote

Fan speed operation has two selectable modes. On this site the units normally operate in Remote mode, where the BMS (Building Management System) controls fan speed across all 21 units based on pressure sensors in the data hall. Operators and technicians should be aware of both modes and what to expect from each.

Local mode The unit controls its own fan speed based on the return air temperature it is measuring. When the return air rises above the setpoint (default 70°F), fans speed up. When it falls, fans slow to minimum (30%). No BMS connection is needed. Used as a fallback if BMS communication is lost.
Remote mode (normal operation) The BMS commands fan speed directly across all 21 units, using data hall pressure sensors to calculate the right speed. The unit follows the BMS command as long as the BMS continues to send a regular heartbeat signal. This is the standard operating mode on this site.
If the BMS loses communication while in Remote mode: By default, the unit holds its last commanded fan speed and generates a communication alarm on the HMI. If you need to take local control during a BMS outage, log in to the HMI (PIN 9675), navigate to the Fans screen, and switch Fan Control Mode to Local. Notify your supervisor and the controls team.

Rounds — what to check on each visit

💧
Drain pan — check for standing water
Inspect the bathtub-style drain pan under each unit on the mechanical gallery floor. Some condensate drainage is normal. Pooled or standing water is not normal — it suggests condensate is not draining, a condensate pump issue, or a more significant water source. Investigate and escalate. Note that the rope leak detector sits in this pan and will alarm if water is present, but do not rely solely on the alarm — a visual check is part of every round.
🔔
HMI — check for active alarms
The 7″ HMI touchscreen is visible from the mechanical gallery on the right side of each right-hand unit. The Alarm Log button in the lower navigation bar displays a red notification badge when any alarm is active or unacknowledged. Check this on every unit during rounds. Common alarms to know: filter DP high, floor leak detector, discharge temp high, fan mismatch (SF1–SF9), and cooling flow. See the Specs & Alarms tab for the full list and first response for each.
▦
Filters — physical inspection
Visually inspect the filter bank on the gallery face of each unit. Look for physical damage (torn media, bent frames, dislodged panels), heavy visible loading (darkened media indicates particle buildup), or collapsed filters that may have been drawn inward by negative pressure. A damaged or missing filter allows unfiltered air to reach the coil, accelerating fouling. The HMI filter alarm is the primary indicator for scheduled replacement, but physical damage requires immediate attention regardless of alarm status.
👂
Abnormal noise or vibration
The fan section should produce a consistent, even airflow sound. Listen and feel for anything unusual: rattling or clicking can indicate a loose component or debris in the fan section; grinding or squealing can indicate a bearing fault in one of the nine ECM fans; intermittent thumping can indicate blade imbalance. Even if the HMI shows all nine fans healthy, unusual noise warrants escalation — the dry-contact fan status only confirms the fan is running, not that it is running cleanly.

Physical layout — what you see in the mechanical gallery

The fan wall units form the physical wall between the mechanical gallery and the data hall. Standing in the mechanical gallery and facing a right-hand unit:

What you see
  • The full filter bank occupies the main face — 4 columns × 4 rows of 24″×24″ filters
  • To the right of the filter bank is the electrical panel enclosure with the 7″ HMI touchscreen mounted on its door
  • The chilled water piping connections enter and exit at the top of the unit
  • At the base of the unit, the bathtub drain pan is visible on the gallery floor, with the rope leak detector cable inside it
  • The EPIV valve is located inside the unit on the coil section and is accessible from the gallery side
What is behind the unit (data hall side)
  • The discharge face of the unit opens directly into the data hall — cooled supply air exits here into the cold aisles
  • Server rack fronts face the cold aisles and draw supply air from the fan wall discharge
  • Hot aisle containment (sliding glass doors) encloses the rear of the racks, directing exhaust air into the return plenum back to the mechanical gallery
Left-hand units: The 3 left-hand units are mirror images of the right-hand units. The electrical panel and HMI are on the left side of the filter bank when facing the unit from the gallery. Handing also affects the orientation of the EPIV and CHW piping assemblies — always confirm before ordering replacement parts.

HMI alarms — first response for operators & technicians
Filter DP high
Filter resistance ≥ 0.81 inH₂O for 5 min. Inspect filters physically. Schedule replacement. Alarm self-resets when DP drops. A clogged filter can reduce airflow and cause downstream temperature alarms.
5 min
Floor leak detector
Moisture detected in the drain pan or a wiring fault. Respond immediately — identify the source. Check the drain pan visually. Escalate. Do not attempt to reset if residue is present on the rope sensor cable.
Immediate
Discharge temp — high
Discharge air ≥ 80°F (default) for 5 min. Check the Cooling screen on the HMI — note the valve output % and cooling flow reading. Check CHW supply is active. Escalate if the unit is not responding.
5 min
Return temp — high
Return air ≥ 120°F (default) for 5 min. Indicates the unit is not keeping up with heat load. Check fan operation on the Fans screen. Check for discharge temp alarm simultaneously. Escalate.
5 min
Cooling flow
Measured CHW flow is outside the expected range for the current valve command, sustained 5 min. Check CHW pump status and whether the unit's isolation valve is open. Escalate to controls/mechanical team.
5 min
Supply fans SF1–SF9
Individual fan mismatch — that specific fan failed to confirm it is running, or lost run status. The unit continues operating on the remaining fans. Note the fan number and log for maintenance follow-up. Escalate if multiple fans alarm.
30 s
External comm alarm
BMS communication to this unit has been lost while in Remote mode. The unit is holding its last fan speed. Notify the controls team. If local temperature control is needed, switch to Local mode at the HMI (service login required).
2 min watchdog
General alarm (GEN-A)
Active whenever any alarm is active. Shown as a red badge on the Alarm Log button in the HMI lower navigation bar. Navigate to the Alarm screen to identify the specific condition.
Immediate

Always confirm handing before ordering replacement parts that are orientation-specific, including EPIVs and piping assemblies.
