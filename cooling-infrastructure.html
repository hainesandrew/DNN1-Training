What this guide covers
This guide covers how heat is removed from the data hall and the wider site — the full cooling loop from rooftop chillers through chilled water distribution, fan walls, airflow management, and supplemental liquid cooling for high-density racks. For how power reaches the mechanical cooling equipment, see the Power Distribution guide.
The fluid — glycol-water mixture
The CHWPs circulate a glycol-water mixture — not plain water. The site's chilled water loop runs a blend of 27 % glycol to 73 % water. The glycol lowers the freezing point so the fluid won't freeze in the rooftop piping during cold weather, and it inhibits corrosion inside the pipe network. This mixture is what flows through the entire chilled water system — from the rooftop chillers, through the CHWPs, down to the fan wall coils and other site loads, and back again.
What the chillers supply
The 24 rooftop chillers serve the entire site — not just the data hall. They supply chilled glycol-water to the fan walls (server cooling), building air conditioning (office, common areas), and everything else on site that requires cooling. The chilled water system is the single source of cooling for all thermal loads across the facility.
Two cooling methods in the data hall
Every rack on site is air-cooled by the fan walls. All 36 server rows receive chilled air through open cold aisles, with hot aisle containment capturing exhaust heat on the rear side. This is the primary cooling system for the entire data hall.

The outer 24 high-density rows also receive supplemental glycol liquid cooling. These rows run high-density AI/compute workloads that generate far more heat per rack than air alone can handle. A glycol-water mixture circulates directly to heat exchangers at the rack, supplementing air cooling by capturing heat at the source.

The inner 12 rows are air-cooled only. These rows house the client's core scientific network servers. They produce less heat per rack and do not require liquid cooling — fan wall air is sufficient.
The cooling loop — summary
Rooftop chillers (ACCs) chill the glycol-water mixture and reject heat to the outside air → CHWPs (also on the roof, next to the chillers) pump the chilled mixture down into the building → fan walls draw warm room air across coils carrying the chilled mixture, cooling the air → cooled air enters open cold aisles → servers pull it in through the front, exhaust hot air out the back → hot aisle containment captures the exhaust → the warmed glycol-water returns to the rooftop chillers and the cycle repeats. For high-density rows, supplemental liquid cooling adds a second heat-removal path directly at the rack.

24 units on the roof · 3 per mechanical skid
roof
There are 24 air-cooled chillers (ACCs) on the roof — 9 critical (served by MI skids) and 15 non-critical (served by MH skids). Each mechanical E-Skid owns exactly 3 chillers. Chilled water pumps (CHWP)
24 pumps · rooftop · 3 per mechanical skid
roof
The 24 CHWPs are located on the roof, directly next to the chillers. Each mechanical E-Skid owns 3 CHWPs — one per chiller. The pumps circulate a 27 % glycol / 73 % water mixture from the chillers down through the building's piping distribution to the fan wall coils, building A/C, and all other site cooling loads. data hall

Open cold aisles
Cooled air delivered to rack fronts
air cooling
Fan walls supply cold air into open cold aisles — the front-facing aisles where server intake grilles are located. "Open" means the cold aisle is not physically enclosed; cool air floods in from the fan walls and servers draw it in with their own internal fans. All 36 rows — both high-density and network — receive their air supply through these open cold aisles.

Server racks

load
Servers draw cold air in through the front of the rack and exhaust hot air out the rear. This front-to-back airflow pattern is why cold aisle / hot aisle separation is critical — it keeps cold supply air and hot exhaust separated, preventing recirculation and ensuring servers receive air at the correct inlet temperature. Hot aisle containment
Captures & isolates exhaust heat
containment
The rear-facing hot aisles are physically enclosed with panels and ceiling structures so that hot server exhaust cannot mix back into the cold supply air. This is the most important aspect of airflow management on the floor. Without containment, hot exhaust recirculates to rack intakes, raising inlet temperatures and reducing cooling effectiveness across the entire hall. Hot aisle containment runs along all 36 server rows.

How each component works — tap to expand
❄️
Air-cooled chiller (ACC)
Refrigerant cycle · heat rejection to outside air
▼

A chiller works like a large, industrial refrigerator. Inside, a refrigerant — a fluid with a very low boiling point — circulates through a closed loop of four stages.

First, warm glycol-water returning from the building passes through the evaporator, where the refrigerant absorbs heat from the fluid and boils into a gas. The now-chilled glycol-water is sent back into the building. The refrigerant gas is then compressed by the compressor, which raises its temperature and pressure significantly. The hot, high-pressure gas flows through the condenser coils on the outside of the unit, where large fans blow outdoor air across the coils — the heat transfers from the refrigerant into the air and the refrigerant condenses back into a liquid. Finally, it passes through an expansion valve, dropping in pressure and temperature, and returns to the evaporator to absorb more heat.

Because the heat is rejected directly to the outside air via the unit's own fans, these are called air-cooled chillers — no cooling towers or condenser water loops are required. The 24 ACCs serve the entire site — fan walls, building A/C, and all other cooling loads.

💧
Chilled water pump (CHWP)
Circulates glycol-water mixture from roof to loads
▼

The CHWPs are centrifugal pumps that create the pressure differential needed to circulate the chilled glycol-water mixture from the rooftop chillers down through the building's pipe network to the fan wall coils, building A/C, and all other cooling loads, and then return the warmed fluid back up to the chillers.

The fluid they pump is a 27 % glycol / 73 % water blend. The glycol lowers the freezing point — critical for rooftop piping exposed to cold weather — and inhibits corrosion inside the pipe network.

Each pump is paired one-to-one with a chiller — 3 CHWPs per mechanical skid, one per chiller. If a pump fails, its corresponding chiller's cold fluid cannot reach the building. The pumps run continuously whenever their chiller is active. They are located on the roof, directly next to the chillers, minimising the distance between the chiller output and the pump inlet.

🌀
Fan wall
Air-to-fluid heat exchange · serves all 36 rows
▼

A fan wall is a large bank of fans arranged in a flat array — typically a full wall section. The fans draw warm room air (returning from the hot aisles) across coils carrying the chilled glycol-water mixture — finned tube heat exchangers with the cold fluid flowing through them.

As the warm air passes across the coils, heat transfers from the air into the glycol-water. The now-cooled air is pushed into the open cold aisles at the front of the server rows. The warmed fluid returns through the piping loop back to the rooftop chillers.

There are 21 fan walls on site. Each is dedicated to its mechanical E-Skid and is not shared across skids. Fan walls are the primary cooling delivery mechanism for the data hall — every one of the 36 server rows depends on them.

🔥
Hot aisle containment
Physical exhaust isolation · all 36 rows
▼

Hot aisle containment is a physical enclosure — panels, doors, and ceiling structures — built around the rear-facing aisles where servers exhaust hot air. The purpose is to completely prevent hot exhaust from mixing back into the cold supply air.

Without containment, hot air recirculates: it drifts over rack tops or around the ends of rows and gets pulled back into server intakes. This raises inlet temperatures, forces servers to spin their fans harder, and reduces the cooling system's overall effectiveness. In severe cases it can cause thermal shutdowns.

With containment in place, hot exhaust is captured and directed back toward the fan walls, where it passes across the chilled glycol-water coils again. The cold aisle stays cold, the hot aisle stays isolated, and the cooling loop operates as efficiently as possible.

🧪
Supplemental liquid cooling
Direct-to-rack cooling · outer 24 rows only
▼

Modern AI accelerators (GPUs, TPUs) produce significantly more heat per rack than traditional servers — often 10–30× the watts per rack. Air cooling alone cannot remove heat fast enough at these densities.

Supplemental liquid cooling addresses this by circulating a glycol-water mixture through heat exchangers mounted directly at or inside the rack. The liquid absorbs heat at the source — right where the hottest components are — and carries it away through piping back into the chilled water system, where the heat is ultimately rejected at the rooftop chillers.

A glycol fill tank on the south side of the building stores the mixture for top-offs and refills. This supplemental system is present only on the outer 24 high-density rows. The inner 12 core scientific network rows have no liquid cooling connections and are air-cooled only.

🏗️
Chilled water tanks
Thermal buffer & system stabilisation
▼

Two chilled water tanks are located on the south side of the building, to the east of GEN-AD and the glycol fill tank. These tanks serve as thermal buffer and pressure stabilisation for the chilled glycol-water loop.

They hold a reserve of chilled fluid that smooths out fluctuations — if demand suddenly spikes (for example, a large number of servers ramping up simultaneously), the tanks provide an immediate supply of cold fluid while the chillers adjust their output. They also help maintain stable system pressure across the piping network.

Where cooling infrastructure is located
❄️ Roof — ACCs & CHWPs
24 air-cooled chillers — 9 critical (MI), 15 non-critical (MH). Each mechanical E-Skid owns 3. The chillers reject heat to the outside air via built-in fans and supply cooling for the entire site — fan walls, building A/C, and all other loads.

24 CHWPs sit directly next to the chillers on the roof. One pump per chiller. They circulate the 27 % glycol / 73 % water mixture from the chillers down into the building's piping distribution.
🌀 Data hall floor — fan walls & containment
21 fan walls supply cooled air into open cold aisles across all 36 server rows. Hot aisle containment encloses all rear aisles. The outer 24 high-density rows also have supplemental liquid cooling connections at the rack level.
🧪 South side — glycol fill tank
Storage tank for the glycol-water mixture used across the cooling system. Located on the south side next to GEN-AD (the house load generator). Used to top off or refill the cooling loop as needed.
🏗️ South side — chilled water tanks
2 chilled water tanks — thermal buffer and pressure stabilisation for the chilled glycol-water system. Located to the east of GEN-AD and the glycol fill tank on the south side.
⚡ North side — mechanical E-Skids
All 8 mechanical E-Skids (3 MI + 5 MH) and their 3 MI generators are on the north side, alongside the electrical skids. Each skid has its own dedicated transformer. For full details on the power path, see the Power Distribution guide.

Key terms
ACC (Air-cooled chiller)
Rooftop refrigeration unit. Uses a refrigerant cycle (evaporator → compressor → condenser → expansion valve) to chill the glycol-water mixture and reject heat to outside air via built-in fans. 24 on site, 3 per mechanical skid. Supplies cooling for the entire site — fan walls, building A/C, and all other loads.
CHWP
Chilled water pump. Centrifugal pump on the roof, next to the chillers. One per chiller. Circulates the 27/73 glycol-water mixture from the roof down to fan wall coils, building A/C, and all other cooling loads. 24 on site.
Glycol-water mixture
The fluid in the chilled water loop: 27 % glycol to 73 % water. Glycol lowers the freezing point (protecting rooftop piping in cold weather) and inhibits corrosion inside the pipe network. This is what flows through the entire chilled water system.
Fan wall
Large fan array that draws warm room air across chilled glycol-water coils and pushes cooled air into open cold aisles. Primary cooling delivery for all 36 server rows. 21 on site, dedicated per mechanical skid.
Cold aisle (open)
Front-facing aisle between rack rows. Not physically enclosed. Fan walls supply chilled air here; servers draw it in through front intake grilles.
Hot aisle containment
Physical enclosure (panels, doors, ceiling structures) around rear-facing aisles. Captures hot server exhaust and prevents it from mixing with cold supply air. Present on all 36 rows.
Supplemental liquid cooling
Direct-to-rack cooling using glycol-water circulated through rack-mounted heat exchangers. Captures heat at the source. Outer 24 high-density rows only; inner 12 network rows are air-cooled only.
Glycol fill tank
Storage tank on the south side (next to GEN-AD) holding glycol-water mixture for topping off or refilling the cooling loop.
Chilled water tanks
Two thermal buffer/pressure stabilisation tanks on the south side. Smooth out fluctuations in cooling demand and maintain stable system pressure in the glycol-water loop.
MI skid
Critical mechanical E-Skid. Generator backed. Each owns 3 rooftop ACCs, 3 rooftop CHWPs, and dedicated fan walls. 3 on site.
MH skid
Non-critical mechanical E-Skid. No generator backup. Same equipment responsibilities as MI. 5 on site.
Refrigerant cycle
The four-stage loop inside a chiller: evaporator (absorbs heat, liquid → gas) → compressor (raises pressure/temp) → condenser (rejects heat, gas → liquid) → expansion valve (drops pressure/temp) → repeat.
Cannibalization row
A smaller row at the front of the data hall with no customer load. Reserve of spare components (GPUs, NICs, drives, etc.) for fast swap into a faulted production rack.
Core scientific network servers
The client's critical network servers in the inner 12 centre rows. Air-cooled only (no liquid cooling). Generator backed via PI E-Skids.
