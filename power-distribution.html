What this guide covers
This guide covers how power enters the site from the utility grid, how it is transformed and conditioned, and how it reaches every load on site — from the IT racks in the data hall to the mechanical cooling plant on the roof, to the office and building services inside the main building. The cooling infrastructure guide (separate document) covers what happens after power reaches the mechanical equipment.

Power flow — utility to load (tap each stage)
⚡
Power poles — utility grid
25 utility feeds into the site
25 kV
High-voltage AC arrives at the site from the utility grid via overhead power poles at 25 kilovolts (kV). There are 25 utility feeds — 24 serving E-Skids and 1 feeding the house/admin load inside the main building. Each feed is independent: one transformer per feed.
🔌
Transformer (×25)
Step-down: 25 kV → 415 V (skids) / 480 V (house)
415 / 480 V
Each transformer steps down 25 kV using electromagnetic induction — no direct electrical connection between input and output. The 24 skid transformers step down to 415 V. The 25th transformer (house load) steps down to 480 V and feeds a dedicated electrical room on the west side of the main building rather than an external skid.
🖥️
E-Skid (×24)
Self-contained power conditioning unit
415 V
Each E-Skid is a modular, skid-mounted assembly housing its own switchboard, UPS units, and output distribution. There are 16 electrical skids (PI and PH) feeding IT racks and 8 mechanical skids (MI and MH) feeding cooling plant. Every skid has a dedicated transformer — nothing is shared between skids.
⬛
Switchboard (SWBD)
Distribution & bypass point
415 V
The switchboard distributes 415 V from the transformer to the UPS inputs within the skid. It uses busbars (thick copper bars) to carry the full current, with individual breakers tapping off to each UPS. It is also the tie point for the ERMS maintenance bypass — an alternate path that keeps the load energised if the UPS needs to be taken offline. Electrical skids feed 2 UPS inputs; mechanical skids feed 3.
⚙️ Maintenance bypass (ERMS)
Manual alternate path around the UPS. Keeps the load energised during planned maintenance. No conditioning or battery backup while active.
🔋
UPS (double-conversion)
Elec: 2 × 1,250 kVA · Mech: 3 × 250 kVA
415 V
Every UPS on site is a double-conversion system: incoming AC is converted to DC (rectifier), then back to AC (inverter). The battery bank sits on the internal DC bus between these two stages. During normal operation, the rectifier powers the DC bus and keeps batteries charged. If AC input fails, batteries take over the DC bus instantly — the inverter keeps running and the load sees no interruption.
💾 DC bus + battery bank
Internal DC link connecting rectifier, batteries, and inverter. Batteries float here and take over instantly if AC input fails.
🔄
Static bypass
Automatic fault transfer — internal to UPS
internal
A solid-state switch inside each UPS that monitors inverter output. If the inverter fails or goes out of tolerance, it transfers the load to the raw utility feed in under 1 ms — fast enough that connected equipment never notices. This is fully automatic and requires no operator action. It is completely separate from the ERMS maintenance bypass, which is manual and planned.
📶
Inverter output
Clean, regulated 415 V AC
415 V AC
The inverter synthesises clean, regulated 415 V AC from the DC bus. Because the output waveform is generated internally rather than passed through from the utility, the load is fully isolated from upstream disturbances — voltage sags, surges, frequency drift, and noise are all eliminated before power reaches any equipment.
Output splits by skid type
⚡ Electrical skids
PI & PH → RPPs → IT racks
156 RPPs
Conditioned power flows from the UPS output through a switchboard to Remote Power Panels (RPPs) on the data hall floor. Each RPP has a 400 A main breaker and distributes power to IT racks via branch circuits. There are 156 RPPs site-wide, all fed by the 16 electrical skids (4 PI + 12 PH). 🌀 Mechanical skids
MI & MH → cooling plant
Direct feed
Mechanical skids feed cooling equipment directly — there are no RPPs in the mechanical path. Each mechanical skid powers its own chillers and chilled water pumps (both on the roof) and fan walls (data hall floor). MI skids serve critical cooling; MH skids serve non-critical cooling. The cooling infrastructure guide covers these systems in detail.

How each component works — tap to expand
🔌
Transformer
Electromagnetic voltage conversion · 25 kV → 415 V / 480 V
▼

A transformer has no moving parts. It works through magnetism — there is no direct electrical connection between the input and output sides.

Inside are two coils of wire wrapped around an iron core. Electricity flowing through the first coil (primary) creates a magnetic field in the core. That magnetic field then generates a voltage in the second coil (secondary) — this is Faraday's Law: a changing magnetic field induces a voltage in a nearby conductor.

The output voltage depends on the ratio of turns (loops) between the two coils. More turns on the primary than the secondary means the voltage comes out lower. The 24 skid transformers reduce 25 kV to 415 V. The house transformer reduces 25 kV to 480 V. Each transformer's MVA rating describes its apparent power capacity — how much load it can support — which is separate from the voltage level.

⬛
Switchboard (SWBD)
Power distribution and protection
▼

A switchboard takes one large incoming power feed and splits it into multiple smaller circuits, each with its own breaker. It's essentially a controlled junction box.

The main incoming power connects to busbars — thick copper bars that carry the full current. Individual circuit breakers tap off those bars and feed downstream equipment. If a fault occurs on one circuit, its breaker trips and isolates just that circuit — everything else keeps running.

In the E-Skid, the switchboard is also where the ERMS maintenance bypass connects. This gives operators a point to route power around the UPS when it needs to be taken offline for maintenance.

🔋
UPS — double-conversion overview
AC → DC → AC power conditioning
▼

Every UPS on this site is a double-conversion system. Incoming AC is converted to DC (by the rectifier), and then back to AC (by the inverter) before it reaches any equipment. This two-step process is where the name comes from.

Because the output is a freshly generated waveform — not the utility supply passed straight through — any noise, sags, surges, or frequency deviations in the incoming power are eliminated. Equipment downstream always receives clean, stable power.

The battery bank sits on the internal DC bus between the two conversions. During normal operation the batteries are fully charged and idle. If the incoming AC supply fails, the batteries immediately take over supplying the DC bus — the inverter keeps running without any gap, and downstream equipment notices nothing.

〰️
Rectifier
AC → DC conversion
▼

AC power alternates direction rapidly — it flows forward, reverses, flows forward again, many times per second. DC power flows in one direction only. The rectifier converts AC into DC.

It does this using diodes — components that only allow current to flow one way. By arranging diodes so both the forward and reverse parts of the AC wave are redirected the same way, the output always flows in one direction. The result is DC power that feeds the internal DC bus and keeps the batteries charged.

📶
Inverter
DC → AC conversion
▼

The inverter does the opposite of the rectifier — it takes DC from the internal bus and produces AC power for the load.

It uses electronic switches that turn on and off very rapidly, chopping the DC into a pattern that, when smoothed out, produces a clean AC waveform at the correct voltage and frequency. This is called pulse-width modulation (PWM).

Because the inverter builds its own output from scratch, the power reaching the load has no connection to whatever is happening on the utility supply. Voltage dips, spikes, or noise upstream have no effect on what the equipment receives.

🔄
Static bypass
Automatic fault transfer — internal to UPS
▼

The static bypass is an automatic escape route built into every UPS. If the UPS develops a fault and can no longer supply the load safely, the static bypass connects the load directly to the incoming utility supply instead — keeping equipment powered while the UPS problem is dealt with.

It uses a solid-state electronic switch with no moving parts, which means it can operate in under one millisecond — fast enough that connected equipment doesn't register any interruption.

This happens automatically — no operator action required. It is completely separate from the ERMS maintenance bypass, which is a manual, planned procedure. The static bypass is a fault response; the ERMS is a maintenance tool.

⚙️
ERMS / Maintenance bypass
Manual bypass — operator initiated
▼

The ERMS is a manual bypass switch that routes power around the UPS so the UPS can be safely taken offline — without cutting power to the equipment it normally supplies.

Before the UPS is disconnected, the bypass feed is confirmed live and matched to the UPS output. The load is then transferred across with no gap in supply. This is a deliberate, operator-controlled action — not automatic.

While on ERMS bypass, the load is running on raw utility power with no conditioning and no battery backup. If the utility supply has an issue during this time, the load will be affected. ERMS operation is always a planned activity, carried out carefully and for the shortest time necessary.

⚡
Remote Power Panel (RPP)
Final distribution to rack loads — electrical skids only
▼

The RPP is the last distribution point before power reaches individual server racks. It receives a single feed from the upstream switchboard and splits it into multiple smaller circuits — one per rack or group of racks.

Each circuit has its own breaker. If there is a fault on one rack feed, only that breaker trips — everything else in the panel stays on. The 400 A main breaker at the top of the panel protects the whole panel and the cable feeding it.

RPPs also give technicians a local isolation point — power to a specific rack can be locked off at the RPP without having to go back to the skid. There are 156 RPPs on this site, all on electrical skids. Mechanical skids feed cooling plant directly and have no RPPs.

Physical layout — where power infrastructure is located
⚡ North side — E-Skids & generators
All 24 E-Skids and 7 of the 8 generators (3 MI + 4 PI) are located on the north side. Each skid has its own dedicated transformer stepping 25 kV down to 415 V. This consolidates the majority of the site's power conditioning and backup generation in one area.
🔌 West side — house electrical room
The 25th transformer steps 25 kV down to 480 V and feeds a dedicated electrical room on the west side of the main building. Inside: the admin UPS, switchgear, and all electrical infrastructure for the house load (office, lighting, HVAC, access control, fire systems, BMS). This is the only power infrastructure housed inside the building rather than in an external skid.
📶 Data hall floor — RPPs
All 156 RPPs are distributed throughout the data hall, fed by the 16 electrical E-Skids (4 PI + 12 PH). Each RPP is the final distribution point before power reaches individual IT racks. Technicians can isolate individual rack feeds at the RPP without returning to the skid.
⛽ South side — GEN-AD & tanks
GEN-AD — the single house load generator — is located on the south side near the loading dock. It backs up the west-side electrical room inside the building and is the only generator not on the north side. Also on the south side: the glycol fill tank (next to GEN-AD) and 2 chilled water tanks (to the east of GEN-AD). These are covered in more detail in the cooling infrastructure guide.
🔗 East & west sides — MMRs
2 Meet Me Rooms (MMRs) — one east, one west. MMRs are where external data carriers physically connect to the site. All data entering and leaving the data center passes through an MMR. While not power infrastructure, they are critical network points to be aware of.

Key terms
E-Skid
Modular, skid-mounted electrical unit housing the switchboard, UPS units, and output distribution in one assembly. 24 on site, each with its own dedicated transformer (25 kV → 415 V).
SWBD
Switchboard — distributes transformer output to multiple UPS inputs via busbars and individual breakers. Also the ERMS maintenance bypass tie point.
UPS
Uninterruptible Power Supply — double-conversion system (rectifier → DC bus → inverter) that protects loads from outages, sags, surges, and frequency drift. 57 total on site.
Double-conversion
UPS topology where incoming AC is converted to DC and then back to AC. Output is a freshly generated waveform, fully isolated from upstream disturbances.
DC bus
Internal DC link connecting rectifier, battery bank, and inverter inside each UPS. Batteries float here and take over instantly on AC failure.
Rectifier
Converts AC to DC using diodes. Feeds the DC bus and keeps batteries charged during normal operation.
Inverter
Converts DC to AC using pulse-width modulation. Synthesises clean, regulated output from the DC bus. Load is fully isolated from upstream.
Static bypass
Automatic solid-state switch inside the UPS. Transfers load to utility in under 1 ms if the inverter fails. No operator action required.
ERMS
Manual maintenance bypass path around the UPS. Operator-initiated during planned maintenance. Load runs on raw utility power — no conditioning, no battery backup.
RPP
Remote Power Panel — 400 A main breaker panel distributing power to IT racks via branch circuits. 156 on site, electrical skids only. Provides local isolation points for technicians.
PI / PH / MI / MH
E-Skid codes. P = power (electrical), M = mechanical. I = critical (generator backed), H = non-critical (no backup). PI and MI are the protected systems.
House load (AD)
Admin/building load: office, lighting, HVAC, access control, fire, BMS. Fed by the 25th transformer (25 kV → 480 V) into a dedicated electrical room on the west side — not a skid. Backed by GEN-AD on the south side.
MVA rating
Apparent power capacity of a transformer — how much load it can support. Separate from the voltage level (which is the step-down ratio).
Busbar
Thick copper bar inside a switchboard that carries the full incoming current. Individual circuit breakers tap off the busbar to feed downstream equipment.
MMR
Meet Me Room — where external data carriers physically connect to the site. 2 on site (east and west). All data in/out passes through an MMR.
Study tip — three power paths: (1) Electrical: 25 kV → transformer (415 V) → SWBD → 2× UPS → RPP → IT rack. (2) Mechanical: 25 kV → transformer (415 V) → SWBD → 3× UPS → chillers / pumps / fan walls. (3) House: 25 kV → transformer (480 V) → west-side electrical room → admin UPS → office / lighting / BMS. Know which skid codes are on each path — PI/PH for electrical, MI/MH for mechanical — and which are generator backed (PI, MI, AD). Remember: 7 skids are on generator, plus the house load (which is not a skid).
